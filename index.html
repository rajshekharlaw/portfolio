Chapter One

The Boy Who Asked Why

Deoghar, Jharkhand

Every story has a setting, and mine begins in Deoghar — a temple town in Jharkhand where tradition runs deep and questions are usually answered with "because that's how it is." That answer never worked on me.

I was the science student who kept wandering into arguments about society. Physics and mathematics trained my logic at St. Xavier's, but the questions that kept me up at night were never about equations. They were about rules. Who makes them? Who do they serve? And who gets left out when they're written carelessly?

Some people inherit politics. Some inherit business. I inherited curiosity — and it turned out to be the most useful asset of all.

When it came time to choose a path, I chose law. Not as a fallback, not as a family tradition, but as the one discipline where the questions I cared about were the entire syllabus.

Chapter Two

Five Years of Fire

NUSRL, Ranchi · 2019–2024

Law school is where most people learn the law. I treated it as a place to stress-test ideas in public. If there was a debate, I entered it. If there was a stage, I took it. If there was a journal, I wrote for it.

By graduation, the scoreboard read: 90+ published articles across platforms like JURIST, Oxford Political Review, and The Fletcher Forum. Best Orator. Winner of the Inter-University Constitutional Quiz. A Millennium Fellow, selected from a pool of over thirty thousand applicants worldwide.

But the numbers were never the point. The point was discovering my voice — the realisation that a well-built argument, delivered at the right moment, could move a room. And that rooms, eventually, move countries.

I didn't just attend law school. I used it — every podium, every byline, every late-night editorial fight.

I also learned to build. Convenor of the Literary Society. Founding member of the Centre for Research in ADR. Part of the body that drafted the Student Bar Association's constitution. Institutions, I learned, don't build themselves.

Chapter Three

The Golden Cage

Khaitan & Co · Nishith Desai Associates · Bengaluru

The conventional summit of an Indian law graduate's ambition is a desk at a top-tier firm. I reached it. At Khaitan & Co, one of India's most storied law firms, I advised the architects of the country's digital economy — FinTech startups, payment companies, digital lenders — on RBI regulations, data privacy, and compliance.

The work was sharp. The stakes were real. Every memo I wrote helped someone navigate the rulebook of India's financial system. At Nishith Desai Associates before that, I had dissected investment funds and cross-border structures. I was good at reading the rules.

And then the question that changed everything: I can read the playbook all my life. But who is in the room where it's written?

Corporate law gave me precision, discipline, and a front-row view of how regulation shapes markets. But a front-row seat is still a seat in the audience. I wanted to be on the other side of the table — where the rules are made, debated, and rewritten. So I did the unthinkable for a young associate on a good trajectory: I walked.

2024–2025

Chapter Four

The World Calls

G20 · Y20 India · KAS · FCDO · Commonwealth

The pivot wasn't a leap into the dark — it was a runway I had been building all along. When India assumed the G20 Presidency, I was already inside the machinery: a Policy and Research Associate at the Y20 India Secretariat, the official youth engagement group of the G20.

There, I co-authored White Papers on behalf of India — on the Future of Work, Climate Change, Peacebuilding, Youth in Democracy, and Health. Documents that carried the country's voice into global negotiation rooms. At the Y20 Summit in Varanasi, I served as Liaison Officer, watching diplomacy happen at handshake distance.

Then came the recognitions that confirmed the new direction: selection as one of 26 Rising Leaders nationwide by the Konrad-Adenauer-Stiftung, Germany's premier political foundation. Research for the UK's Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office on comparative refugee law across six jurisdictions. Engagement with the Commonwealth Youth Council on ASEAN governance dialogues.

Policy stopped being a subject I wrote about. It became a room I was invited into — in Delhi, in Varanasi, and beyond India's borders.

Today, as Regional Coordinator for Central Asia and Eastern Europe at the Indian Youth Diplomacy Forum, I keep one foot in that world — because the best domestic policy is written by people who have seen how the rest of the world writes theirs.

Chapter Five

The Corridors of Power

Nation First Policy Research Centre · New Delhi · Now

Today I work where I always wanted to be: at the Parliament and Legislative Affairs Division of the Nation First Policy Research Centre — the layer of Indian democracy where bills are dissected line by line, where a single clause can decide the fate of an industry, and where research quietly becomes law.

My days are spent in legislative research, parliamentary process analysis, and evidence-based policy recommendations. The corporate lawyer in me reads every bill the way a market would. The policy researcher in me asks what it means for the citizen. The debater in me makes sure the argument survives the committee room.

Legislation is as much about persuasion and process as it is about drafting. The best policies are the ones that survive scrutiny beyond conference rooms.

This is not the end of the story. It's the part where the training, the publications, the pivot, and the world stage converge into a single sentence: I help write the rules of the republic — and I'm just getting started.
